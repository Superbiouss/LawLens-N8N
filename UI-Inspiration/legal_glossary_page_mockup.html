Liquidated damages

liq·ui·dat·ed dam·ag·es

Damages Liability Contract enforcement

"A fixed sum agreed in advance that one party pays if they break a specific obligation — the amount is set regardless of what the actual loss turns out to be."

Complexity
High
Legal context
Liquidated damages clauses are common in commercial contracts as a way to agree upfront on compensation for specific breaches — avoiding costly litigation over how much harm was actually suffered. They're valid if the amount was a genuine pre-estimate of likely loss at the time of signing. Courts will strike them down as unenforceable "penalty clauses" if they're clearly punitive rather than compensatory.
Jurisdiction notes
In India: Section 74 of the Indian Contract Act limits courts to awarding actual proven loss, regardless of the contract amount. In the US (Delaware): enforceable if it was a reasonable pre-estimate of harm. The phrase "regardless of actual harm" weakens enforceability in both.
Example in a contract
"Any breach shall result in liquidated damages of $500,000 per incident, regardless of actual harm suffered by the Disclosing Party."
The phrase "regardless of actual harm" is a red flag — it transforms a compensatory clause into a punitive penalty, which courts in India and many US jurisdictions may refuse to enforce.
